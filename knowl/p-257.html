In light of PropertyÂ 6, we see that for any rational number \(r\text{,}\) \(E(r)=e^r\text{.}\) Not only does this give us the series representation \(e^r=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{1}{n!}r^n\) for any rational number \(r\text{,}\) but it gives us a way to define \(e^x\) for irrational values of \(x\) as well. That is, we can define
\begin{equation*} e^x=E(x)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{1}{n!}x^n \end{equation*}
