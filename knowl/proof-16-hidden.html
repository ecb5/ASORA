We could use the definition of continuity to prove Theorem 9.27, but Theorem 9.21 makes our job much easier. For example, to show that \(f+g\) is continuous, consider any sequence \(\left(x_n\right)\) which converges to \(a\text{.}\) Since \(f\) is continuous at \(a\text{,}\) then by Theorem 9.21, \(\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}f(x_n)=f(a)\text{.}\) Likewise, since \(g\) is continuous at \(a\text{,}\) then \(\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}g(x_n)=g(a)\text{.}\)

By Theorem 7.18 of Chapter 7,\(\)

\begin{alignat*}{1} \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}(f+g)(x_n)\amp=\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \left(f(x_n)+g(x_n)\right)\\ \amp =\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}f(x_n)+\,\lim_{n \rightarrow\infty}g(x_n)\\ \amp =f(a)+g(a)\\ \amp =(f+g)(a)\text{.} \end{alignat*}

Thus by Theorem 9.21, \(f+g\) is continuous at \(a\text{.}\) The proof that \(f\cdot g\) is continuous at \(a\) is similar.
