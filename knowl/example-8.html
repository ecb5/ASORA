Example 7.3.2.

Consider the sequence, \(\left(n\right)_{n=1}^\infty\text{.}\) This clearly diverges by getting larger and larger \(\ldots\) Ooops! Let’s be careful. The sequence \(\left(1-\frac1n\right)_{n=1}^\infty\) gets larger and larger too, but it converges. What we meant to say was that the terms of the sequence \(\left(n\right)_{n=1}^\infty\) become arbitrarily large as \(n\) increases.
This is clearly a divergent sequence but it may not be clear how to prove this formally. Here’s one way.
To show divergence we must show that the sequence satisfies the negation of the definition of convergence. That is, we must show that for every \(r\in\RR\) there is an \(\eps>0\) such that for every \(N\in\RR\text{,}\) there is an \(n>N\) with \(\left|n-r\right|\ge\eps\text{.}\)
So let \(\eps=1\text{,}\) and let \(r\in\RR\) be given. Let \(N=r+2\text{.}\) Then for every \(n>N\) \(\abs{n-r} > \abs{(r+2)-r}=2>1\text{.}\) Therefore the sequence diverges.
