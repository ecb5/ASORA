We will outline the proof of part (a) of TheoremÂ 3.0.11 and indicate how it can be used to prove part b.
Let \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) be real numbers with \(\alpha > \beta\text{.}\) There are two cases.
  • Case 1:.
    \(\alpha-\beta > 1\text{.}\) In this case there is at least one integer between \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\text{.}\) Since integers are rational we are done.
  • Case 2:.
    \(\alpha-\beta \le 1\text{.}\) In this case, by the Archimedean Property there is a positive integer, say \(n\text{,}\) such that \(n(\alpha-\beta) = n\alpha-n\beta > 1\text{.}\) Now there will be an integer between \(n\alpha\) and \(n\beta\text{.}\) You should now be able to find a rational number between \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\text{.}\)
